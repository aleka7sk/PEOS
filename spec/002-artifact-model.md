# PEOS-002 — Artifact Model

**Category:** Normative

**Status:** Draft

**Version:** 0.1

---

# Abstract

This document defines the normative Artifact Model of PEOS.

It establishes the identity, revision, representation, origin, relationship, and integrity semantics of Artifacts.

Artifacts are the primary persistent representation of Engineering State in PEOS. The model defined by this specification allows engineering knowledge to remain identifiable, reproducible, inspectable, and independent of any particular runtime, storage system, file format, or development tool.

---

# Purpose

The purpose of the Artifact Model is to define what qualifies as an Artifact within PEOS and which conditions must remain true throughout its existence.

This document defines:

* Artifact identity;
* Artifact Types;
* Artifact Revisions;
* revision immutability;
* Artifact content;
* Artifact Representations;
* Artifact metadata;
* Artifact origin;
* provenance and engineering justification;
* Artifact Relations;
* the Artifact Graph;
* Artifact integrity;
* extension and conformance requirements.

This document does not prescribe a physical storage model, serialization format, repository structure, or development methodology.

---

# Scope

This specification applies to every entity represented as an Artifact within a conformant PEOS engineering system.

Examples may include:

* product requirements;
* constraints;
* architectural descriptions;
* design models;
* source code;
* schemas;
* implementation plans;
* tests;
* validation results;
* review records;
* deployment configurations;
* operational observations;
* Decision Records;
* imported engineering references.

An entity does not qualify as a PEOS Artifact solely because it is stored in a file, database, repository, or engineering tool.

It qualifies as an Artifact when it satisfies the applicable requirements of this specification.

# Normative Foundation

This document depends on:

* PEOS-000 — Overview;
* PEOS-001 — Philosophy.

The terms Product, Product Engineering, Engineering State, Artifact, Decision, Runtime, Validation, Contract, Invariant, and Transformation are used according to their normative definitions in those specifications.

# Artifact Model Overview

The Artifact Model distinguishes between:

* an Artifact as a persistent logical engineering identity;
* an Artifact Revision as a fixed state of that Artifact;
* an Artifact Representation as an encoding or projection of a Revision;
* an Artifact Relation as an explicit connection between identifiable engineering subjects.

Conceptually:

```text
Artifact
    has identity
    has type
    has one or more Revisions

Artifact Revision
    represents a fixed Artifact state
    contains or references content
    has origin and provenance
    may have one or more Representations

Artifact Relation
    connects Artifacts or Artifact Revisions
    has an explicit type and direction
```

An Artifact is the unit of engineering identity.

An Artifact Revision is the unit of reproducibility.

---

# Artifact

An Artifact is an identifiable and persistent representation of engineering knowledge, intent, evidence, or Product state.

An Artifact MAY represent information that was:

* created within the engineering system;
* imported from an external source;
* derived from another Artifact;
* produced by a Transformation;
* recorded from an observation;
* generated by a Runtime;
* authored by a human;
* produced through combined human and automated activity.

An Artifact MUST have:

* a stable Artifact Identifier;
* an Artifact Type;
* at least one identifiable Artifact Revision before it participates in normative Engineering State.

An Artifact MAY exist before its first Revision is formally recorded, but such an Artifact MUST NOT be treated as reproducible or validated.

An Artifact MUST NOT be defined solely by its current physical location.

Moving, copying, or re-encoding an Artifact MUST NOT, by itself, change its logical identity.

---

# Artifact Identity

Every Artifact MUST have a stable logical identity within its applicable Product scope.

The identity MUST distinguish the Artifact from every other Artifact within that scope.

Artifact identity MUST remain stable across:

* revisions;
* changes of representation;
* changes of storage location;
* changes of runtime;
* migrations between conformant tools.

An Artifact Identifier MAY be:

* globally unique;
* unique within a Product;
* unique within a defined engineering namespace.

The applicable uniqueness scope MUST be explicit.

An Artifact Identifier MUST NOT be reused for an unrelated Artifact.

An Artifact that replaces another Artifact but represents a different logical engineering subject MUST receive a different Artifact Identifier.

A correction, refinement, or evolution of the same logical engineering subject SHOULD normally produce a new Artifact Revision rather than a new Artifact.

---

# Artifact Type

Every Artifact MUST have an Artifact Type.

An Artifact Type defines the engineering role, expected semantics, and applicable contracts of an Artifact.

Examples of Artifact roles may include:

* requirement;
* constraint;
* architecture;
* design;
* implementation;
* test;
* evidence;
* operational record;
* review;
* Decision Record.

This list is illustrative and is not a closed type system.

An Artifact Type definition MUST:

* have a stable identity;
* state its intended engineering meaning;
* identify any additional invariants applicable to its Artifacts;
* remain consistent with this specification;
* avoid redefining normative concepts owned by another PEOS specification.

PEOS implementations MAY define additional Artifact Types.

An extension Artifact Type MUST preserve all general Artifact invariants defined by this document.

A type name alone is insufficient to establish normative meaning.

The type definition or the applicable contract MUST be identifiable.

---

# Artifact Revision

An Artifact Revision is an identifiable and fixed state of an Artifact.

Every recorded Artifact Revision MUST have:

* a Revision Identifier;
* a reference to its Artifact;
* content or an identifiable content reference;
* creation provenance;
* an identifiable origin or explicit origin status;
* an integrity identity;
* an explicit revision status.

A Revision Identifier MUST uniquely identify the Revision within the identity scope of its Artifact.

A reference to a specific Artifact state MUST identify a specific Artifact Revision.

A reference to an Artifact without identifying a Revision refers only to the logical Artifact and MUST NOT be interpreted as a reproducible reference to a particular state.

---

# Revision Immutability

A recorded Artifact Revision MUST be immutable.

After a Revision has become part of persistent Engineering State:

* its normative content MUST NOT be altered;
* its meaning MUST NOT be silently reinterpreted;
* its integrity identity MUST continue to identify the same content and metadata scope.

Any material change to the normative state of an Artifact MUST produce a new Artifact Revision.

Material changes include changes to:

* normative content;
* engineering meaning;
* applicable constraints;
* accepted assumptions;
* declared origin;
* relationships that are part of the Revision itself;
* metadata included in its integrity scope.

Correction of storage corruption MAY restore the original Revision when the restored state is demonstrably identical to the recorded state.

Correction MUST NOT be used to replace one valid Revision state with another.

A tool MAY maintain mutable working content before a Revision is recorded.

Mutable working content is not a recorded Artifact Revision and MUST NOT be represented as immutable Engineering State.

---

# Revision Ordering

Revisions of an Artifact MAY have an explicit ordering.

Revision ordering MAY be expressed through:

* sequence numbers;
* timestamps;
* predecessor relationships;
* version identifiers;
* another deterministic mechanism.

An implementation MUST NOT assume that Revision Identifiers are inherently sortable unless their governing contract explicitly guarantees ordering semantics.

A new Revision MAY:

* refine a previous Revision;
* correct a previous Revision;
* supersede a previous Revision;
* branch from a previous Revision;
* represent a parallel valid state.

The Artifact Model does not require every Artifact to have a single linear revision history.

Branching Revision histories are permitted.

The structural and engineering semantics of Artifact Supersession are defined by this specification.

Any Lifecycle transition or Lifecycle consequence associated with Supersession is governed by PEOS-003 and by the applicable Lifecycle Definition.

---

# Artifact Content

Artifact content is the engineering information represented by an Artifact Revision.

Content MAY be:

* directly embedded;
* referenced through an immutable content address;
* stored in an external system;
* composed from multiple identifiable components;
* represented through another conformant mechanism.

Content MUST be retrievable or otherwise inspectable when required by the applicable contract.

When content cannot be retained or disclosed, the Revision MUST preserve enough metadata to identify:

* that the content existed;
* why it is unavailable;
* the integrity identity of the retained or removed state when permitted;
* the applicable retention, security, or legal condition.

A content reference MUST identify the intended content with enough precision to avoid silent resolution to a different state.

A mutable location alone is not a reproducible content reference.

For example, a reference to a repository branch, current web page, or mutable database row is insufficient when a specific historical state is required.

---

# Artifact Representation

An Artifact Representation is a physical or logical encoding through which an Artifact Revision can be stored, transmitted, rendered, inspected, or processed.

Examples include:

* a Markdown document;
* an OpenAPI document;
* source code;
* a database record;
* a diagram;
* a serialized object;
* generated HTML;
* a binary package;
* a remote API resource.

An Artifact Revision MAY have multiple Representations.

Multiple Representations MAY refer to the same Revision when they preserve the same normative engineering meaning.

A change of Representation MUST NOT, by itself, create a new Artifact Revision.

A new Revision MUST be created when re-representation changes normative content or meaning.

A Representation MUST identify:

* the Revision it represents;
* its representation format or mechanism;
* any transformation required to obtain its normative content;
* whether it is authoritative, derived, partial, or rendered.

A rendered or generated Representation MUST NOT silently replace its authoritative source unless the applicable contract explicitly permits it.

When multiple Representations disagree, the applicable contract MUST identify the authoritative interpretation or require resolution of the conflict.

---

# Artifact Metadata

Artifact metadata describes an Artifact or Artifact Revision without being identical to its primary engineering content.

Metadata MAY include:

* title;
* description;
* ownership;
* authorship;
* timestamps;
* classification;
* sensitivity;
* retention policy;
* status;
* tags;
* applicable contracts;
* integrity information.

Metadata that affects normative interpretation MUST be included in the normative state of the applicable Artifact Revision.

Non-normative operational metadata MAY be changed without creating a new Revision when such change does not alter engineering meaning, traceability, validation, or reproducibility.

The boundary between normative and operational metadata MUST be defined by the applicable Artifact Type or contract.

---

# Artifact Status

Every recorded Artifact Revision MUST expose an explicit status sufficient to prevent unfinished, invalidated, unavailable, or superseded states from being mistaken for an applicable current state.

This document does not define a universal closed set of lifecycle statuses.

An applicable lifecycle MAY define statuses such as:

* draft;
* recorded;
* reviewed;
* accepted;
* rejected;
* invalidated;
* superseded;
* deprecated;
* withdrawn.

Status meaning and valid status transitions are defined by PEOS-003 or by a conformant specialized lifecycle.

A status change that modifies the normative applicability of a Revision MUST be persistent and traceable.

A runtime MUST NOT infer normative acceptance solely from the existence of a Revision.

---

# Artifact Origin

Every significant Artifact Revision MUST have an identifiable engineering origin.

Origin explains the engineering basis from which the Revision exists.

An origin MAY include:

* one or more Artifact Revisions;
* one or more Decisions;
* an external constraint;
* an authorized instruction;
* an observation;
* a Transformation;
* accepted evidence;
* a formally declared independent creation.

Origin MUST be sufficiently explicit to distinguish justified creation from unexplained existence.

A Revision MAY have multiple origins.

A Revision created without another Artifact as input MUST still identify the authority, observation, instruction, or condition that justified its creation.

When origin is unknown, unavailable, disputed, or reconstructed, that condition MUST be explicit.

An implementation MUST NOT present reconstructed origin as original provenance without qualification.

---

# Provenance and Engineering Justification

PEOS distinguishes provenance from engineering justification.

Provenance answers:

> Who or what created, recorded, or transformed this Revision, when, and through which mechanism?

Engineering justification answers:

> On what engineering basis should this Revision exist or be considered applicable?

Every recorded Artifact Revision MUST have identifiable provenance.

Provenance SHOULD identify:

* the responsible Actor or Runtime;
* the creation or recording time;
* the producing Transformation when applicable;
* the relevant tool or mechanism when required for reproducibility.

Every significant Artifact Revision MUST have identifiable engineering justification.

Engineering justification MAY refer to:

* requirements;
* constraints;
* Decisions;
* evidence;
* prior Artifact Revisions;
* external obligations;
* accepted instructions.

A statement identifying only the generating tool or AI model is provenance, not sufficient engineering justification.

A statement identifying only the engineering reason without recording the producing actor or mechanism is justification, not complete provenance.

Provenance and justification MAY be represented through Artifact Relations rather than duplicated directly in every Revision, provided that the required information remains persistent and inspectable.

---

# External Artifacts

An External Artifact is an Artifact whose authoritative content or identity is governed outside the local PEOS implementation.

Examples may include:

* laws;
* regulatory standards;
* vendor documentation;
* external APIs;
* customer-provided specifications;
* third-party research;
* externally managed source repositories.

An External Artifact MAY participate in Engineering State.

A reference to an External Artifact MUST identify the applicable external state with sufficient precision for its intended use.

When an external source is mutable, the PEOS implementation SHOULD preserve:

* an immutable snapshot;
* a version identifier;
* a content digest;
* an effective date;
* another reproducible reference.

An external source MUST NOT be assumed to remain unchanged.

The local system MUST distinguish between:

* externally authoritative content;
* a locally retained representation;
* a local interpretation of that content.

A local interpretation is a separate Artifact or Revision when it introduces engineering meaning not present in the external source itself.

---

# Artifact Relation

An Artifact Relation is an explicit, typed, and directed connection between identifiable Artifact subjects.

A relation subject MAY be:

* an Artifact;
* an Artifact Revision;
* another entity explicitly permitted by a later normative PEOS specification.

Every Artifact Relation MUST identify:

* its source;
* its target;
* its Relation Type;
* its provenance;
* its applicable scope when the scope is not self-evident.

A relation connecting specific engineering states MUST reference Artifact Revisions rather than only logical Artifacts.

A relation to a logical Artifact MAY be used when the relationship intentionally applies to the Artifact across its Revisions.

The distinction between Artifact-level and Revision-level relations MUST be preserved.

---

# Relation Type

A Relation Type defines the semantic meaning and structural constraints of an Artifact Relation.

Examples may include:

* derived-from;
* refines;
* implements;
* verifies;
* supersedes;
* depends-on;
* conflicts-with;
* references.

These examples do not establish their complete normative semantics.

The normative semantics and additional integrity constraints of specialized engineering Relation Types are defined by the applicable specialized PEOS specification or Product contract.

Cross-artifact traceability paths, traceability coverage, completeness policies, and orphan detection remain the responsibility of a future Traceability Model.

Every Relation Type definition MUST state:

* its semantic meaning;
* permitted source subjects;
* permitted target subjects;
* whether its source-to-target direction carries normative meaning;
* whether multiple relations are permitted;
* whether cycles are permitted;
* whether the relation applies to Artifacts, Revisions, or both;
* any integrity constraints.

An implementation MAY define extension Relation Types.

An extension Relation Type MUST NOT silently reuse the identity of a normative PEOS Relation Type with different semantics.

---

# Artifact Graph

The Artifact Graph is the directed typed graph formed by Artifacts, Artifact Revisions, and their Relations.

The Artifact Graph represents structural connections within Engineering State.

The Artifact Graph is not required to be globally acyclic.

Cycle validity depends on the applicable Relation Types.

A Relation Type MAY:

* permit cycles;
* prohibit cycles;
* require cycle detection;
* classify cycles as warnings;
* impose additional graph constraints.

For example, a supersession relation may require acyclicity while a reference relation may allow cycles.

The Artifact Graph MUST preserve enough identity to distinguish:

* logical Artifacts;
* individual Artifact Revisions;
* relations between logical identities;
* relations between fixed states.

The Artifact Graph SHOULD support inspection from both source and target directions.

A future Traceability Model defines cross-artifact traceability paths, traceability coverage, completeness policies, and orphan detection.

Specialized PEOS specifications MAY define domain-specific relationship obligations without becoming the general Traceability Model.

---

# Artifact Operations

PEOS implementations MAY perform operations including:

* creation;
* recording;
* revision;
* derivation;
* representation;
* relation creation;
* status transition;
* invalidation;
* supersession;
* retention;
* removal.

Every operation that changes persistent Engineering State MUST be represented as an observable Transformation or as another persistent state change permitted by a normative PEOS specification.

Operations MUST preserve the invariants of this document.

---

# Artifact Creation

Artifact creation establishes a new logical Artifact identity.

Creation MUST NOT be used when the intended result is only a new state of an existing Artifact.

Creation MUST record or enable the recording of:

* the Artifact Identifier;
* the Artifact Type;
* applicable scope;
* initial provenance.

An Artifact MUST have a first recorded Revision before its content participates in normative Engineering State.

---

# Revision Operation

A Revision Operation creates a new fixed state of an existing Artifact.

A Revision operation MUST:

* preserve the Artifact Identifier;
* create a new Revision Identifier;
* preserve the previous recorded Revision;
* identify its predecessor or revision basis when applicable;
* record its provenance and origin;
* establish a new integrity identity.

Revision MUST NOT overwrite the previous Revision.

---

# Artifact Derivation

Derivation creates an Artifact or Artifact Revision using another identifiable engineering subject as an input.

A derived Artifact Revision MUST identify its derivation origin.

Derivation MAY produce:

* a new Revision of the same Artifact;
* a new Artifact;
* a new Representation of an existing Revision.

The producing mechanism MUST distinguish these outcomes.

A generated rendering that preserves normative meaning is normally a Representation.

A generated object with independent engineering identity or meaning is normally a new Artifact.

A transformed state of the same logical engineering subject is normally a new Revision.

---

# Artifact Supersession

Artifact Supersession is an Artifact Relation establishing that one identifiable Artifact subject replaces another identifiable Artifact subject within a defined scope.

Every individual Supersession relationship SHALL be binary.

Every Supersession relationship SHALL identify:

- exactly one superseding source subject;
- exactly one superseded target subject;
- the applicable scope;
- its provenance;
- the governance basis or authority under which replacement was established, where required by applicable governance;
- the resulting Lifecycle consequence, if any.

The source-to-target direction SHALL be from superseding subject to superseded subject.

A Supersession relationship MAY connect:

- one Artifact Revision to another Artifact Revision;
- one Artifact to another Artifact;
- another compatible participant combination explicitly permitted by a specialized normative PEOS specification.

A relation connecting replacement of specific represented engineering states SHALL identify the applicable Artifact Revisions.

A specialized PEOS specification SHALL define any permitted cross-level Supersession relationship between an Artifact and an Artifact Revision.

One superseding subject MAY participate as the source of multiple Supersession relationships.

One superseded subject MAY be the target of multiple Supersession relationships only where their applicable scopes remain explicitly distinguishable and non-contradictory.

Supersession cycles SHALL NOT be permitted.

An Artifact or Artifact Revision SHALL NOT directly or transitively supersede itself.

Supersession SHALL be explicit.

Creation of a newer Artifact Revision SHALL NOT automatically imply that an earlier Artifact Revision is superseded, invalid, withdrawn, or non-applicable.

Supersession SHALL NOT:

- erase the superseded subject;
- merge the identities of the superseding and superseded subjects;
- delete historical Artifact Revisions;
- silently rewrite historical Engineering State.

A Supersession relationship does not by itself require a Lifecycle transition.

Where Supersession causes or requires a Lifecycle transition, State Assignment, status change, or other Lifecycle consequence, that consequence SHALL be governed by PEOS-003 and the applicable Lifecycle Definition.

A Lifecycle State, State Assignment, Transition, status value, archival action, or newer Revision SHALL NOT by itself establish which subject supersedes another subject or the scope of replacement.

Traceability consequences associated with Supersession remain governed by the applicable specialized PEOS specification, Product contract, or future Traceability Model.

---

# Artifact Invalidation

Invalidation indicates that an Artifact Revision MUST NOT be relied upon within its affected scope.

Invalidation MUST:

* be explicit;
* identify the invalidated Revision;
* state or reference the reason;
* identify the responsible authority or producing Transformation;
* preserve the invalidated Revision as historical Engineering State when retention permits.

Invalidation does not rewrite the historical fact that the Revision existed or was previously used.

If invalidation affects dependent Artifacts, the impact MUST be representable through applicable Relations and Validation rules.

---

# Artifact Removal and Retention

Removal of Artifact content MUST NOT silently rewrite Engineering State.

A conformant implementation MAY remove or restrict content because of:

* retention policy;
* confidentiality;
* security;
* privacy;
* intellectual property;
* legal obligation;
* storage constraints;
* external unavailability.

When a recorded Artifact Revision has participated in normative Engineering State, removal SHOULD preserve a tombstone or equivalent persistent record.

The retained record SHOULD identify:

* Artifact identity;
* Revision identity;
* former existence;
* removal or restriction reason;
* removal time;
* responsible authority;
* remaining integrity information when permitted.

Relations to removed content MUST NOT silently resolve to a different Artifact or Revision.

A removed identifier MUST NOT be reused for unrelated engineering content.

---

# Evidence

Evidence is an engineering role that an Artifact may perform.

An Artifact serves as Evidence when it supports or contradicts an engineering claim, Decision, Validation result, or quality assessment.

Evidence MAY include:

* test results;
* benchmarks;
* experiments;
* review records;
* operational observations;
* formal analyses;
* accepted external facts.

Being classified as Evidence does not automatically establish reliability, sufficiency, validity, or acceptance.

The conditions under which an Artifact qualifies as valid Evidence for a Validation are defined by PEOS-006.

---

# Decision Records

A Decision is a domain concept defined by PEOS-004.

A Decision Record is an Artifact that persistently represents a Decision.

A Decision MUST NOT be reduced to an Artifact Type when doing so would remove the Decision semantics defined by PEOS-004.

A Decision Record MUST conform to:

* this Artifact Model;
* the Decision Model;
* any applicable Template Contract.

A Decision and its Decision Record MAY have different identifiers.

The relationship between them MUST be explicit when they are modeled separately.

---

# Artifact Integrity

Artifact integrity is the ability to establish that an Artifact Revision and its applicable normative metadata remain the state they claim to represent.

Every recorded Artifact Revision MUST have an integrity identity.

An integrity identity MAY be established through:

* a cryptographic digest;
* an immutable version identifier;
* a content-addressed reference;
* an append-only record;
* a trusted repository commit;
* another mechanism providing equivalent assurance.

The integrity mechanism MUST be appropriate to the applicable risk and conformance profile.

An integrity identity MUST define or reference the scope it protects.

The scope MAY include:

* content;
* normative metadata;
* Representation;
* relations embedded in the Revision;
* provenance information.

An integrity identity that protects only content MUST NOT be represented as protecting unverified metadata.

An implementation MUST be able to distinguish:

* verified integrity;
* unverifiable integrity;
* failed integrity;
* integrity not yet evaluated.

A failed integrity check MUST prevent the affected Revision from being silently treated as valid.

# Artifact Invariants

A conformant implementation MUST preserve the following invariants.

## Identity Invariant

Every Artifact has one stable logical identity within its declared scope.

## Revision Identity Invariant

Every recorded Artifact Revision has an identity unique within its Artifact.

## Immutability Invariant

A recorded Artifact Revision does not change.

Material change creates a new Revision.

## Reproducibility Invariant

A reference intended to identify a fixed Artifact state identifies a specific Revision.

## Origin Invariant

Every significant Artifact Revision has an identifiable engineering origin or an explicit qualified statement that its origin is unknown or unavailable.

## Provenance Invariant

Every recorded Artifact Revision has identifiable creation or recording provenance.

## Representation Independence Invariant

Artifact identity and Revision identity do not depend solely on physical format, storage location, or runtime implementation.

## Relation Identity Invariant

Every Artifact Relation identifies its subjects and Relation Type.

## Relation Semantics Invariant

A relation derives its meaning from an identifiable Relation Type definition.

## Supersession Identity Invariant

Every Supersession relationship explicitly identifies one superseding source subject, one superseded target subject, and the scope of replacement.

## Supersession Acyclicity Invariant

Supersession cycles are prohibited.

## Supersession and Lifecycle Separation Invariant

Supersession remains distinct from Lifecycle State, State Assignment, Transition, and status.

A Lifecycle construct SHALL NOT by itself establish Supersession.

A Supersession relationship SHALL NOT be required to produce a Lifecycle consequence.

## History Preservation Invariant

Revision, supersession, invalidation, migration, or removal does not silently rewrite historical Engineering State.

## Integrity Invariant

Every recorded Artifact Revision has an integrity identity whose protection scope is identifiable.

## Extension Invariant

Extension Artifact Types and Relation Types preserve the general requirements of this specification.

# Extension Model

PEOS implementations MAY extend the Artifact Model through:

* additional Artifact Types;
* additional Relation Types;
* specialized metadata;
* specialized integrity mechanisms;
* specialized lifecycle bindings;
* specialized storage and representation models.

An extension MUST:

* have an identifiable namespace or ownership boundary;
* preserve normative PEOS semantics;
* avoid collisions with normative PEOS identifiers;
* identify any additional constraints;
* remain distinguishable from the normative core.

An extension MUST NOT weaken a normative requirement while claiming full conformance.

An implementation MAY support a nonconformant extension mode, but that mode MUST be explicitly identified as nonconformant or partially conformant.

---

# Runtime Independence

The Artifact Model is independent of any specific Runtime.

A Runtime MAY:

* create Artifacts;
* create Revisions;
* transform content;
* establish Relations;
* calculate integrity identities;
* evaluate Artifact invariants;
* manage Representations.

A Runtime MUST NOT:

* redefine Artifact identity semantics;
* mutate recorded Revisions;
* silently change relation meaning;
* treat mutable references as fixed Revisions without qualification;
* erase engineering history while claiming preservation;
* redefine extension behavior as normative PEOS behavior.

Runtime obligations are defined by PEOS-008.

---

# Storage Independence

PEOS does not require Artifacts to be stored:

* in a single repository;
* in a file system;
* in Git;
* in a database;
* in a PEOS-specific service;
* under a single organizational owner.

A distributed Artifact system is conformant when the required identity, Revision, origin, relation, integrity, and inspection semantics remain satisfied.

Physical co-location is not required.

Logical identifiability is required.

---

# Conformance

An implementation conforms to this specification when every entity it presents as a PEOS Artifact satisfies the applicable requirements of this document.

Conformance requires, at minimum:

* stable Artifact identity;
* identifiable Artifact Type;
* immutable recorded Revisions;
* reproducible Revision references;
* identifiable provenance;
* identifiable engineering origin for significant Revisions;
* explicit relation identity and semantics;
* preservation of engineering history;
* identifiable integrity scope;
* clear distinction between normative and extension behavior.

An implementation MUST NOT claim full Artifact Model conformance when it cannot preserve recorded Revision identity or immutability.

A tool that processes only selected Artifact Types MAY conform within a declared limited scope.

Its conformance statement MUST identify that scope.

---

# Non-Goals

This document does not define:

* mandatory Artifact Types for every Product;
* physical Artifact schemas;
* JSON, YAML, Markdown, or database representations;
* a universal repository layout;
* lifecycle states or transition rules;
* Decision structure;
* mandatory traceability chains;
* validation algorithms;
* quality metrics;
* Runtime APIs;
* Template formats;
* organizational ownership models;
* access control or authorization policy;
* retention durations.

These concerns are defined by later PEOS specifications, implementation profiles, or Product-specific contracts.
